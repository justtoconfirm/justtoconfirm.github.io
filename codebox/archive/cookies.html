Cookies

Cookies are small text files stored on a computer used to store information in web pages. When a browser requests a web page from a server, cookies belonging to the page are added to the request. It allows the website to recognise a user's device and store some information about the user's preferences or past actions.

The cookie property of the Document object lets you read and write cookies.

  • You must tell people if you set cookies and clearly explain what the cookies do and why.
  • You must get the person's consent to store cookies on their device.
  • There is an exception for cookies that are essential to provide an online service at someone's request, such as to remember items in an online basket or to ensure security in online banking.

As long as you do this the first time you set cookies, you do not have to repeat it each time the same person visits the website. The device however maybe used by different people, so you may want to consider repeating the process at suitable intervals (perhaps set an expiry date for the cookie).

PECR (The Privacy and Electronic Commincations Regulations) sits alongside the Data Protection Act and GDPR.

The ICO (Information Commissioner's Office) have a page dedicated to Cookies and similar technologies on their website.

There are two exemptions from PECR which apply:

  • Cookies used to remember goods a user wishes to buy when they add goods to their online basket or proceed to the checkout during online shopping & e-commerce transactions. Cookies used to remember preferences are also excluded.
  • Session cookies providing security that is essential to comply with data protection security requirements, such as online banking.
  • load-balancing cookies that ensure the content of the page loads quickly and effectively by distributing the workload across multiple servers/machines.

It is still good practice to provide users with information about these cookies, even if you do not need consent.

Cookies applied for Marketing, Analytics or any form of tracking of user behaviour must not be set by default and must be opt-in by the user before they can be applied.

First & Third-party Cookies

First-party cookies are directly stored by the website (or domain) you visit. These cookies allow the collection of analytics data, set language settings, and perform other useful functions to provide a good user experience. First-party cookies are often used in eCommerce for shopping carts to allow items to be remembered until the user reaches the checkout.

Third-party cookies are created by domains that are not the website (or domain), a third-party. A third-party cookie can be set by a third-party server, such as VE or other vendors. These are usually used for online advertising and are placed on a website through a script or tag. A third-party cookie is accessible on any website that loads the third-party server's code.

To summarise:

  • First-party cookies are placed there by the website you're visiting and are generally useful - they remember if you're logged in or not, for example.
  • Third-party cookies are added to yoir device by other parties the website you're visiting has agreed to use. These types of cookies can be placed in ads, track you as you move around the web and build a profile of the user by gathering data on your browsing history then linking it to an identifier that's attached to the said user. This is used to show you targeted ads across the web.

Both Firefox and Safari have introduced blockers to actively stop third-party cookies from working. In 2022, Google's Chrome will also stop using third-party cookies and replace them with an API called the Privacy Sandbox.

Google is also introducing FLoC (Federated Learning of Cohorts) as a system to replace third-party cookies for advertising. In March 2021, Chrome started testing FLoC in the real-world.

A company such as Amazon may use third-party cookies when a user shops on Amazon. The user may browse a number of items. Later, they may receive an email and other advertisements for the items they were browsing earlier. Even if the user closes their browser and ends the session, that tracking data inside the cookie will still be on their computer.

First-party cookies are supported by all browsers and can be blocked, or deleted, by the user. Third-party cookies are supported by all browsers, but many modern browsers including Firefox and Chrome are blocking the creation of third-party cookies by default. Users are also deleting third-party cookies on their own.

Write a new Cookie

We can create a cookie using document.cookie and set the name and value of this cookie within a string. Cookies are saved in name/value pairs.

    

document.cookie = "username=Dan Jackson";

We can also add an expiry date. By default, a cookie is deleted when the browser is closed.

    

document.cookie = "username=Dan Jackson; expires=Thu, 31 Dec 2020 12:00:00 GMT";

Return all cookies

We can use the cookie property to return all cookies in a single string.

    

let cookies = document.cookie;
